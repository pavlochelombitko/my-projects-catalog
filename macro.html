Macroeconomics

The study of the economy as a whole — output, employment, price levels, and the forces that shape long-run growth.

1. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period. It's the single most widely used measure of economic activity. There are three ways to calculate it:

Expenditure approach: Add up all spending on final goods and services.

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

Where C = Consumption, I = Investment, G = Government spending, X = Exports, M = Imports.

Income approach: Add up all incomes earned in production (wages, profits, rents, interest). Output approach: Sum the value added at each stage of production.

Real vs. Nominal GDP

Nominal GDP measures output using current prices and can be inflated by rising prices alone. Real GDP adjusts for inflation using constant (base-year) prices, giving a truer picture of actual output growth. Always compare real GDP when evaluating economic performance over time.

Figure 1.1: Stylized illustration of nominal vs. real GDP growth. Nominal GDP rises faster because it includes inflation. The shaded area represents a recession — two or more consecutive quarters of declining real GDP.

GDP Components Breakdown

In most developed economies, household consumption is the largest component (typically 60–70% of GDP), followed by government spending, business investment, and net exports. Understanding this composition helps explain why consumer confidence and spending habits have such outsized effects on the economy.

Figure 1.2: Breakdown of GDP by expenditure component. Consumption dominates, which is why consumer confidence indexes are closely watched economic indicators.

2. The Business Cycle

Economies don't grow in a straight line. They fluctuate through recurring periods of expansion and contraction known as the business cycle. Understanding where the economy is in the cycle helps explain policy responses and market behavior.

3. Inflation

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level over time. It reduces the purchasing power of money — the same amount of currency buys fewer goods and services. Moderate inflation (around 2%) is considered healthy; high inflation erodes savings and creates uncertainty.

Inflation Rate = ((CPI this year − CPI last year) / CPI last year) × 100%

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) tracks the cost of a representative basket of goods and services over time. It's the most commonly used measure of inflation.

Types of Inflation

Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand outpaces aggregate supply — "too much money chasing too few goods." Cost-push inflation occurs when production costs rise (e.g., oil price shocks), pushing up prices even without excess demand. Built-in inflation results from workers and firms expecting prices to continue rising, leading to higher wage demands and price increases — a self-fulfilling cycle.

Winners & Losers from Inflation

Losers: Savers (money loses value), fixed-income earners (pensions, bonds), lenders (repaid in less valuable currency). Winners: Borrowers (repay with cheaper money), asset owners (property, stocks rise in nominal terms), governments with fixed-rate debt.

4. Unemployment

The unemployment rate measures the percentage of the labor force that is actively seeking work but cannot find it. Not everyone without a job is unemployed — retirees, students, and discouraged workers are not counted in the labor force.

Unemployment Rate = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100%

Natural Rate of Unemployment

Even in a healthy economy, some unemployment always exists (frictional + structural). This is the natural rate of unemployment, typically estimated at 4–5% in developed economies. When actual unemployment falls below this level, inflationary pressures tend to build.

5. Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply

The AD-AS model is the macroeconomic equivalent of supply and demand. It shows how the overall price level and total output (real GDP) are determined.

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total spending on domestic goods and services at each price level. It slopes downward because higher prices reduce real wealth, increase interest rates, and make exports less competitive.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS) slopes upward — firms produce more when prices rise (and wages are sticky). Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) is vertical at potential GDP — in the long run, output depends on resources and technology, not the price level.

Figure 5.1: The AD-AS model. Long-run equilibrium occurs where AD, SRAS, and LRAS all intersect. At this point, the economy produces at its potential output (Y*) and the price level is stable.

Shifts in AD and AS

An increase in consumer confidence, government spending, or money supply shifts AD right — raising both output and the price level in the short run. A rise in oil prices or wages shifts SRAS left — reducing output while raising prices (this is called stagflation). Technological advances shift LRAS right, representing genuine long-run economic growth.

6. The Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve illustrates the short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment. When unemployment is low, firms compete for workers by raising wages, which pushes up prices. When unemployment is high, there is less wage pressure and inflation falls.

Figure 6.1: The short-run Phillips Curve (SRPC) shows the trade-off between inflation (π) and unemployment (U). The long-run Phillips Curve (LRPC) is vertical at the natural rate — in the long run, there is no trade-off; unemployment returns to its natural rate regardless of inflation.
Policy Implication

The Phillips Curve helps explain why policymakers face a dilemma: stimulating the economy to reduce unemployment may cause inflation, while fighting inflation may increase unemployment. In the long run, the trade-off disappears — sustainable employment gains require structural reforms, not just demand stimulation.
