Monetary & Fiscal Policy

How central banks and governments use their tools to stabilize the economy, manage inflation, and promote employment.

On this page

1. Monetary Policy

Monetary policy refers to actions taken by a central bank (such as the Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, or Bank of England) to influence the money supply and interest rates. The primary goals are price stability (controlling inflation), full employment, and sustainable economic growth.

Key Tools

Interest rate setting: The central bank sets a benchmark interest rate (e.g., the federal funds rate in the US). Lowering rates makes borrowing cheaper, encouraging spending and investment. Raising rates does the opposite, cooling the economy.

Open market operations (OMO): The central bank buys or sells government bonds. Buying bonds injects money into the banking system (expansionary). Selling bonds withdraws money (contractionary).

Reserve requirements: The central bank can change the fraction of deposits that commercial banks must hold in reserve. Lower requirements free up more money for lending.

Quantitative easing (QE): When interest rates hit zero (the "zero lower bound"), central banks can purchase longer-term assets to push down long-term rates and stimulate the economy — an unconventional tool used extensively after the 2008 financial crisis and during COVID-19.

Expansionary vs. Contractionary

Expansionary monetary policy (lower rates, buy bonds) stimulates the economy when it's in recession. Contractionary policy (higher rates, sell bonds) cools an overheating economy to control inflation. Central banks must balance these goals carefully — acting too aggressively in either direction creates problems.

2. The Money Market

The money market model shows how the interest rate is determined by the interaction between money supply and money demand.

Money demand slopes downward — at lower interest rates, the opportunity cost of holding money is lower, so people hold more. Money supply is set by the central bank and shown as a vertical line (it doesn't depend on the interest rate).

4. Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy involves government decisions about spending and taxation to influence the economy. Unlike monetary policy (controlled by the central bank), fiscal policy is determined by elected officials — parliaments and presidents.

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

During a recession, the government can increase spending (on infrastructure, transfers, public services) or cut taxes to boost aggregate demand. The logic: more money in consumers' pockets → more spending → higher output → lower unemployment.

Contractionary Fiscal Policy

When the economy overheats, the government can reduce spending or raise taxes to cool demand and reduce inflationary pressure. This is politically harder to do, since cutting spending and raising taxes are unpopular.

Figure 4.1: Expansionary fiscal policy (increased government spending or tax cuts) shifts AD from AD₁ to AD₂. This closes the recessionary gap, moving the economy toward potential output (LRAS). The price level also rises somewhat.
Crowding Out

A key criticism of fiscal expansion: government borrowing to fund increased spending can raise interest rates, which discourages (or "crowds out") private investment. The net stimulus may therefore be smaller than intended. This effect is stronger when the economy is near full capacity.

5. The Multiplier Effect

When the government spends an extra dollar, the total impact on GDP is greater than one dollar because of the multiplier effect. The recipient of government spending re-spends part of that money, and those recipients spend part of theirs, and so on — creating a chain of spending.

Spending Multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC)

Where MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume) is the fraction of additional income that households spend. If MPC = 0.8, the multiplier is 1/(1−0.8) = 5. This means every $1 of government spending increases GDP by $5.

Figure 5.1: With MPC = 0.8, an initial $100 of government spending generates $500 of total economic activity. Each round re-spends 80% of the previous round, with the series converging to 1/(1−0.8) × $100 = $500.

The tax multiplier is slightly smaller than the spending multiplier because when taxes are cut, households save part of the extra income rather than spending all of it. Tax multiplier = −MPC / (1 − MPC).

6. Monetary vs. Fiscal — Comparison

FeatureMonetary PolicyFiscal Policy
Controlled byCentral bank (independent)Government (elected officials)
Main toolsInterest rates, OMO, QEGovernment spending, taxation
Speed of implementationFast (weeks)Slow (legislative process)
Time lag to effect6–18 monthsShorter once enacted
PrecisionBlunt (economy-wide)Can target specific sectors
Side effectsAsset bubbles, bank profitsCrowding out, budget deficits
Political independenceHigh (shielded from politics)Low (politically driven)
LimitationZero lower bound, liquidity trapDebt sustainability
Policy Mix

In practice, monetary and fiscal policy work best together. During the 2008 crisis and COVID-19 pandemic, both central banks (QE, near-zero rates) and governments (stimulus checks, infrastructure spending) acted simultaneously to prevent deeper recessions. Coordination matters.
