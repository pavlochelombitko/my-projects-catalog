International Trade

How and why countries trade, the gains from specialization, trade barriers, and the foreign exchange market.

On this page

1. Why Countries Trade

International trade allows countries to consume beyond their own production possibilities. By specializing in what they produce most efficiently and trading for the rest, all countries can be better off. The key reasons countries trade include differences in resource endowments, technology, climate, and labor skills.

Absolute advantage means a country can produce a good using fewer resources than another country. But even without absolute advantage, trade is still beneficial — this is the insight behind comparative advantage.

Key Insight

Trade is not a zero-sum game. When countries specialize according to their comparative advantage, total world output increases. Both trading partners can consume more than they could in isolation — trade creates winners on both sides.

2. Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is the ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country. Even if one country is better at producing everything (absolute advantage in all goods), both countries still gain from trade if they specialize in the good where their opportunity cost is lowest.

Example: Two Countries, Two Goods

CountryWheat (tons/year)Cloth (units/year)Opportunity Cost of 1 Wheat
Country A100500.5 Cloth
Country B40401.0 Cloth

Country A has an absolute advantage in both goods (produces more of each). But Country A has a comparative advantage in wheat (gives up only 0.5 cloth per wheat, vs. 1.0 for Country B). Country B has a comparative advantage in cloth (gives up only 1 wheat per cloth, vs. 2 for Country A).

If Country A specializes in wheat and Country B specializes in cloth, total world output increases and both can trade to consume more.

3. Trade Barriers

Despite the benefits of free trade, governments often impose barriers. These include:

Tariffs — taxes on imported goods that raise their price, protecting domestic producers but hurting consumers. Quotas — limits on the quantity of a good that can be imported. Subsidies — payments to domestic producers that make them more competitive against imports. Non-tariff barriers — regulations, licensing requirements, and standards that make importing harder.

Arguments For Protection

Protecting infant industries (new industries that need time to develop), national security concerns (e.g., domestic steel production), protecting jobs in declining industries, and countering unfair practices like dumping (selling below cost to destroy competitors).

Arguments Against Protection

Trade barriers raise consumer prices, reduce choice, protect inefficient firms, invite retaliation from trade partners, and reduce total economic welfare. Most economists favor free trade with targeted safety nets for displaced workers.

5. Exchange Rates

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. Under a floating exchange rate system, the rate is determined by supply and demand in foreign exchange markets.

When demand for a currency increases (e.g., foreign investors want to invest in the country), the currency appreciates (becomes stronger). When demand falls, the currency depreciates (becomes weaker).

Figure 5.1: The foreign exchange market for euros. Increased demand for euros (D€₁ → D€₂) — perhaps from increased exports or foreign investment — causes the euro to appreciate from e₁ to e₂ (each euro now costs more dollars).
Impact of Exchange Rate Changes

Appreciation (stronger currency): Makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper → hurts exporters, benefits importers and consumers. Depreciation (weaker currency): Makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive → helps exporters, hurts import-dependent industries. This is why exchange rates are closely watched by trade-dependent economies.

6. Balance of Payments

The Balance of Payments (BOP) is a record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world over a period. It has two main accounts:

Current Account: Records trade in goods and services, income flows (investment returns, wages), and unilateral transfers (foreign aid, remittances). A current account deficit means the country imports more than it exports.

Capital & Financial Account: Records cross-border investment — foreign direct investment (FDI), portfolio investment (stocks, bonds), and official reserve transactions. A country with a current account deficit must have a corresponding capital account surplus (foreigners are lending to or investing in the country).

Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account = 0
The Twin Deficits

A government budget deficit often accompanies a current account deficit — known as the "twin deficits" hypothesis. Government borrowing raises interest rates, attracting foreign capital, which appreciates the currency and makes exports less competitive. This link between fiscal and trade imbalances is a key topic in open-economy macroeconomics.
