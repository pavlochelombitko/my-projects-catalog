How individuals and firms make decisions, and how markets coordinate those decisions through prices.

1. Supply & Demand

Supply and demand is the most fundamental model in economics. It explains how prices are determined in a market and why they change. Demand represents the quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to buy at each price, while supply represents the quantity producers are willing and able to sell.

Law of Demand

As the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases (and vice versa), holding all else equal. This inverse relationship gives the demand curve its downward slope.

Law of Supply

As the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases (and vice versa), holding all else equal. Higher prices incentivize producers to supply more, giving the supply curve its upward slope.

Figure 1.1: The basic supply and demand model. The demand curve (D, red) slopes downward, the supply curve (S, teal) slopes upward. They intersect at the equilibrium price P* and quantity Q*.

Several factors can shift the demand curve: changes in income, preferences, prices of related goods (substitutes and complements), population, and expectations. Similarly, the supply curve shifts due to changes in input costs, technology, number of sellers, and expectations about future prices.

Shift vs. Movement Along the Curve

A common mistake is confusing a movement along the curve (caused by a price change of the good itself) with a shift of the curve (caused by a change in an external factor). When the price of coffee rises, you move along the demand curve. When incomes rise, the entire demand curve shifts to the right.

Figure 1.2: An increase in demand (D₁ → D₂) shifts the demand curve to the right. This results in a higher equilibrium price and higher equilibrium quantity.

2. Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs where the supply and demand curves intersect — the price at which the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied. At this point, there is no tendency for the price to change.

If the price is above equilibrium, a surplus (excess supply) exists — producers want to sell more than consumers want to buy. This puts downward pressure on prices. If the price is below equilibrium, a shortage (excess demand) exists — consumers want to buy more than producers want to sell, pushing prices upward.

Figure 2.1: At price P₁ (above equilibrium), quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded — a surplus. At P₂ (below equilibrium), quantity demanded exceeds supply — a shortage. The market self-corrects toward equilibrium.
Key Insight

Markets act as self-correcting mechanisms. Surpluses push prices down; shortages push prices up. The equilibrium price is the only price at which the market "clears" — no excess supply or demand remains.

3. Elasticity

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. It tells us whether consumers are sensitive or insensitive to price changes.

PED = (% Change in Quantity Demanded) / (% Change in Price)

If |PED| > 1, demand is elastic — consumers are highly responsive to price changes (e.g., luxury goods, items with many substitutes). If |PED| < 1, demand is inelastic — consumers are less responsive (e.g., necessities like insulin or gasoline). If |PED| = 1, demand is unit elastic.

Figure 3.1: Demand curves with different elasticities. Perfectly inelastic (vertical) — quantity doesn't change with price. Perfectly elastic (horizontal) — any price increase reduces quantity demanded to zero. Most real goods fall between these extremes.

Determinants of Elasticity

Several factors determine how elastic demand is for a good: the availability of close substitutes (more substitutes → more elastic), whether the good is a necessity or luxury, the proportion of income spent on the good, and the time horizon (demand becomes more elastic over time as consumers adjust).

4. Consumer & Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. It represents the net benefit consumers receive from participating in the market. Producer surplus is the difference between the price producers receive and the minimum price they would accept. Together, they make up total economic surplus (or total welfare).

Figure 4.1: Consumer surplus (blue area) is the triangle between the demand curve and the equilibrium price. Producer surplus (green area) is between the supply curve and the equilibrium price. Total surplus is maximized at the free-market equilibrium.
Why This Matters

Economists use surplus analysis to evaluate the welfare effects of government policies like price controls, taxes, and subsidies. A policy that reduces total surplus creates a deadweight loss — economic value that is lost and not transferred to anyone.

6. Market Failures

Markets don't always produce efficient outcomes. Market failure occurs when the free market fails to allocate resources in the most socially efficient way. Key types include:

Externalities are costs or benefits that affect third parties not involved in the transaction. A factory polluting a river imposes a negative externality on downstream communities — the social cost exceeds the private cost. Education creates positive externalities — society benefits beyond just the student.

Figure 6.1: With a negative externality (e.g., pollution), the marginal social cost (MSC) exceeds the marginal private cost (MPC). The market overproduces (Qmarket > Q*), creating a deadweight loss (DWL). A Pigouvian tax can close this gap.

Public goods are non-excludable and non-rival — once provided, no one can be excluded from using them, and one person's use doesn't reduce availability. Examples include national defense and street lighting. Because consumers can "free ride," private markets tend to underprovide public goods.

Information asymmetry occurs when one party in a transaction has more information than the other. This can lead to adverse selection (before a transaction — e.g., used car markets) and moral hazard (after a transaction — e.g., insurance holders taking more risks).
